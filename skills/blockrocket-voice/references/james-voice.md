# James Morgan — voice reference

Source: medium.com/@james.morgan and medium.com/blockchain-manchester (2020–2025). James is the analyst and standards engineer (co-author of EIP-2981). Bio in his own words: "NFT nerd, crypto enthusiast, lover of music, humanist, mostly found hacking web3." X: @jimbob_87.

## Fingerprint

- **Thesis first, then structure.** Opens with a state-of-the-world claim, then breaks it down:
  > "Crypto is in a more mature and defined stage than ever before. While hype cycles still exist, many areas of the industry have signs of product-market fit…"
- **Heavy bold for emphasis** — key phrases bolded mid-sentence, several per paragraph. This is his single most recognisable habit.
- **Repeating scaffolds.** Sections share a pattern, e.g. each ends:
  > "🔹 **Why it's growing:** …"
  And problem sections flip the marker: "🔸 **DAOs** — Governance participation is low…"
- **Owns opinions in first person singular**, even in analytical pieces:
  > "I am bullish on the blend of 'AI & DAOs'"
  > "I would not bet against the casino though."
  > "A continued growing industry which I remain a firm believer in"
- **Hedged speculation, honestly flagged:**
  > "remains to be seen"
  > "Some speculative thoughts on the future …"
  > "The answer is still being written. 🚀"
- **Earnest mission register for announcements:**
  > "When we started KO in 2018 our mission was simple, build out a platform for digital creators to monetise their work"
  > "We truly believe that this will help bring all of us, KO, eBay and the wider community, to another level."
- **Links inline to everything** — every project named gets its URL in parentheses: "**Arbitrum** ([arbitrum.io](https://arbitrum.io))".
- **Signs collective announcements** plainly: "KO Founders".

## Grammar quirks to preserve (never fake new ones)

Comma-spliced mission statements ("our mission was simple, build out a platform…"), the occasional typo left in, long sentences that gather clauses as he thinks.

## What James would never write

Unhedged hype about the future. A claim about a protocol without naming it and linking it. Hiding an opinion behind "some say".
